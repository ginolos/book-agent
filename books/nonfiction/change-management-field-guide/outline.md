# The Change Management Field Guide

## Topic and Keywords

Topic: Change management for leaders, managers, project teams, and internal consultants.

Keywords: change management, organizational change, transformation, stakeholder engagement, resistance, communication plan, sponsor coalition, adoption, training, measurement, operating model, culture, change readiness, change fatigue, implementation, governance, sustainment.

## Detailed 16-Chapter Outline

### Chapter 1: Why Change Fails in Ordinary Work
- Define change management as the work of moving people from current behavior to useful new behavior.
- Explain why many changes fail through unclear purpose, weak sponsorship, overload, and poor reinforcement.
- Introduce the book's practical premise: change succeeds when it is treated as operating work, not a communication event.

### Chapter 2: Start With the Case for Change
- Distinguish business rationale from human rationale.
- Clarify the current problem, future state, consequences of inaction, and tradeoffs.
- Translate executive language into manager and frontline language.

### Chapter 3: Map the Real System
- Identify impacted groups, workflows, incentives, tools, policies, and informal networks.
- Use impact mapping to find hidden work created by the change.
- Separate technical implementation from adoption requirements.

### Chapter 4: Build the Sponsor Coalition
- Define sponsorship as active direction, resource clearing, visible decisions, and repeated reinforcement.
- Identify primary sponsors, local sponsors, and resistant power centers.
- Create sponsor routines that do not depend on personality.

### Chapter 5: Understand Stakeholders Without Stereotyping Them
- Segment stakeholders by impact, influence, readiness, and required behavior.
- Avoid labeling people as difficult when constraints explain their response.
- Build listening loops before communication campaigns.

### Chapter 6: Diagnose Readiness and Risk
- Assess awareness, desire, knowledge, ability, reinforcement, capacity, and trust.
- Identify risks from timing, workload, prior failed change, and leadership credibility.
- Create readiness actions that reduce risk before launch.

### Chapter 7: Design the Change Strategy
- Turn diagnosis into a practical approach across sponsorship, communication, training, support, and reinforcement.
- Choose pacing and sequencing deliberately.
- Define what will be centralized and what must be localized.

### Chapter 8: Communicate for Meaning and Action
- Explain why communication must answer what is changing, why, when, how, and what support exists.
- Build message architecture for different audiences.
- Use repetition, manager enablement, and feedback channels.

### Chapter 9: Equip Managers as the Translation Layer
- Show how managers convert strategy into daily meaning.
- Provide manager briefing packs, talking points, escalation paths, and team discussion guides.
- Protect managers from being surprised by decisions they must explain.

### Chapter 10: Build Capability Through Training and Practice
- Distinguish information, instruction, practice, coaching, and performance support.
- Design training around the tasks people must perform after go-live.
- Use simulations, job aids, and office hours to reduce adoption friction.

### Chapter 11: Work With Resistance
- Treat resistance as data about risk, identity, incentives, workload, or trust.
- Use structured inquiry and visible response.
- Escalate only after the team understands the underlying concern.

### Chapter 12: Manage Change Fatigue
- Recognize overload created by too many simultaneous initiatives.
- Use a change portfolio view to sequence work and protect capacity.
- Retire old work when new work is introduced.

### Chapter 13: Measure Adoption and Value
- Define behavior, usage, proficiency, sentiment, and outcome metrics.
- Build leading and lagging indicators.
- Use measurement to learn and adjust, not merely to prove success.

### Chapter 14: Govern the Implementation
- Establish decision rights, issue escalation, risk review, and dependency management.
- Keep governance close enough to operational reality.
- Use governance to clear obstacles rather than create reporting theater.

### Chapter 15: Sustain the Change
- Move from launch to reinforcement, ownership, process integration, and continuous improvement.
- Embed new behavior in roles, routines, metrics, systems, and recognition.
- Plan hypercare and handoff deliberately.

### Chapter 16: The Change Leader's Operating System
- Present a repeatable operating rhythm for leaders and change practitioners.
- Include checklists, meeting cadence, decision tools, and review questions.
- Close with a pragmatic view of change as disciplined attention to people, work, and value.
