# Chapter 14: Govern the Implementation

Governance is often associated with status meetings, steering committees, and slide decks, but its real purpose is decision quality. Change creates issues that cross functions, budgets, systems, customer commitments, compliance obligations, and authority lines. Without governance, those issues drift until they become adoption problems. People wait for answers, make local exceptions, or quietly return to the previous way of working because the new way feels unresolved.

Good governance keeps the work connected to reality. It clarifies who decides, how risks are escalated, what evidence matters, and how leaders remove barriers. It also protects implementation teams from two common traps: over-controlling every local choice and under-controlling the decisions that shape outcomes. The goal is not to create more ceremony. The goal is to create enough structure that important decisions are made at the right level, at the right time, with the right information.

Implementation governance should be practical. It should help leaders see where adoption is strong, where friction is accumulating, where the plan needs adjustment, and where tradeoffs need explicit ownership. When governance works, meetings become a place where uncertainty is reduced and barriers are removed. When governance fails, meetings become a reporting ritual that consumes the same people who are trying to make the change work.

## Decision Rights

Every significant change needs clear decision rights. Some decisions belong to the executive sponsor, some to process owners, some to technology owners, and some to local leaders. Ambiguity slows implementation because the same issue is reopened repeatedly. It also frustrates teams because employees receive different answers depending on which leader they ask. A change cannot scale if decision authority is hidden inside relationships, seniority, or whoever speaks last in the meeting.

Decision rights should be defined before implementation pressure peaks. The team should identify the categories of decisions likely to arise, such as scope changes, policy exceptions, system configuration, data migration choices, training requirements, role impacts, customer communication, and launch readiness. For each category, define who recommends, who decides, who must be consulted, who must be informed, and who can approve exceptions. Frameworks such as RAPID, RACI, or DACI can help, but the value comes from the conversation, not the acronym.

A decision log should capture the decision, owner, date, rationale, affected groups, evidence considered, expected impact, and communication requirement. This creates memory for the project. It also helps managers explain why a choice was made when employees ask. Without a decision log, implementation teams lose time reconstructing the past, and local leaders may interpret silence as permission to choose their own version of the change.

Practical decision governance includes an expiration point for temporary decisions. Many implementation choices are made under uncertainty. A pilot may require a temporary workaround, a region may need a short-term exception, or a system defect may require manual review for a few weeks. These are not failures if they are named and managed. They become failures when temporary exceptions become permanent operating rules without review.

A useful diagnostic is the "three reopenings" test. If the same decision has been debated three times, governance is weak. The issue may have no clear owner, the rationale may not be understood, or the decision may have been made without the right stakeholders. The remedy is not another general discussion. The remedy is to restate the decision category, confirm the decision owner, document the rationale, and communicate the outcome to the people affected.

### Section Summary
- Clear decision rights prevent repeated debate, delay, and inconsistent local interpretation.
- A decision log preserves rationale and gives managers usable explanations.
- Temporary decisions need review dates so exceptions do not become unmanaged precedent.

## Governance Forums and Cadence

Governance is not one meeting. It is a set of forums with different purposes. A working team may meet weekly to resolve delivery issues. A change leadership group may meet every two weeks to review adoption risk, readiness, and field feedback. A steering committee may meet monthly or at key gates to make tradeoff decisions that require executive authority. Combining all of these needs into one meeting usually produces either excessive detail for executives or superficial discussion for the people doing the work.

Each forum should have a clear mandate. The working team should manage near-term execution, dependency tracking, issue triage, and preparation of decisions. The change leadership group should review adoption evidence, manager readiness, communication needs, resistance patterns, and stakeholder alignment. The steering committee should decide on scope, funding, policy, timing, unresolved cross-functional conflicts, and launch readiness. If a topic does not match the forum mandate, move it to the right forum rather than letting the agenda sprawl.

| Forum | Primary purpose | Typical decisions |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Working team | Resolve delivery issues and prepare decisions | Dependencies, issue triage, action owners |
| Change leadership group | Review adoption evidence and field friction | Readiness actions, communication shifts, manager support |
| Steering committee | Own executive tradeoffs and unresolved conflict | Scope, funding, timing, policy, launch readiness |

Cadence should reflect implementation risk. A low-risk process update may need light governance. A global operating model change, regulatory program, or major technology deployment may need weekly executive attention during critical periods. Cadence can also change over time. A program may move from monthly governance during design to weekly governance during launch preparation, then to daily triage during hypercare. Static cadence often misses the rhythm of actual risk.

Effective agendas separate information from judgment. Pre-reads should carry standard status, milestone updates, and background data. Meeting time should focus on decisions needed, risks requiring intervention, new field intelligence, tradeoffs, and actions that have stalled. This discipline respects the time of senior leaders and implementation teams. It also prevents the meeting from becoming a place where people read slides to one another.

A simple governance architecture can be built around five recurring questions:

- What has changed since the last review?
- Which risks are increasing?
- Which decisions are needed now?
- Where are employees or managers experiencing friction?
- What leadership action would materially improve adoption?

If a governance forum cannot answer these questions, it is probably collecting status rather than governing implementation.

### Section Summary
- Governance works best as a set of forums with distinct purposes and authority levels.
- Cadence should increase or decrease with implementation risk.
- Meeting time should be reserved for judgment, decisions, risk, and leadership action.

### References
- Project Management Institute, governance and program management practices.
- ITIL practices on transition planning, operational readiness, and escalation.
- Harvard Business Review, articles on meeting effectiveness and executive decision discipline.

## Risk and Issue Escalation

Issues should be escalated when they cannot be resolved at the level where they appear. A local training question may not need executive attention. A conflict between sales targets and required process adoption probably does. Governance should define escalation thresholds so teams do not guess. Without thresholds, some teams escalate everything to protect themselves, while others absorb serious risks until there is no time left to act.

Escalation criteria should include four tests:

- Impact: how many people, customers, processes, or obligations are affected.
- Urgency: how quickly the issue must be resolved to prevent damage.
- Authority: whether the team facing the issue has the power to solve it.
- Pattern: whether the issue is isolated or a signal of a broader design flaw.

A single user complaint may be local. The same complaint from five regions may indicate that the training, process, or tool does not fit the work.

Risk reviews should include adoption risk, not only technical or schedule risk. If a project is green on build milestones but red on manager readiness, the overall status is not green. Leaders need a view that reflects the full implementation. Adoption risk may appear as low training confidence, unclear role ownership, process conflict, poor data quality, high workaround usage, low manager engagement, unresolved policy tension, or sentiment that the change is impractical.

The risk register should be written in plain language. A vague entry such as "stakeholder resistance" is not actionable. A useful entry would say, "Regional service managers are not reinforcing the new dispatch process because their performance scorecard still rewards speed over first-time resolution." This statement identifies the group, the behavior, the cause, and the governance problem. It points toward a possible intervention: align the scorecard, clarify expectations, and equip managers to handle the tradeoff.

Escalation should not be treated as failure. Mature implementation teams escalate early because they understand that some issues require authority, funding, or cross-functional compromise. Leaders should reward well-formed escalation: clear facts, options, recommendation, decision needed, and consequence of inaction. Poor escalation is different. It arrives as a general complaint with no analysis, no owner, and no request. Governance should teach teams the difference.

### Section Summary
- Escalation thresholds should be based on impact, urgency, authority, and pattern.
- Adoption risk belongs beside schedule, cost, technical, compliance, and operational risk.
- Strong escalation frames the decision needed and the consequence of inaction.

- Project Management Institute, risk and issue management practices.

## Evidence for Governance Decisions

Governance decisions should be evidence based, but evidence should be interpreted carefully. Implementation data is often incomplete, delayed, or influenced by local conditions. A low training completion rate may mean employees are disengaged, but it may also mean shift schedules were not accommodated. A spike in support tickets may indicate tool failure, but it may also indicate that people are trying to use the new process and need normal launch support. Governance should ask what the evidence means before deciding what to do.

Useful evidence includes adoption metrics, operational metrics, manager feedback, employee sentiment, support tickets, quality checks, customer impact, exception requests, and direct observation. No single source is sufficient. Surveys may reveal confidence but not actual behavior. System usage may show activity but not quality. Manager feedback may be rich but biased by local pressure. Triangulation reduces the risk of overreacting to one data point.

A practical governance dashboard should be small enough to support discussion. It should show leading indicators, lagging indicators, open decisions, top risks, issue aging, readiness by group, and adoption by segment. It should also show where data is missing. Missing data is a governance signal because leaders cannot manage what they cannot see. If the team cannot tell whether a critical employee group is ready, that uncertainty should be treated as a risk, not as a footnote.

Evidence should be segmented by the way work is actually performed. Enterprise averages hide variation. A training completion rate of 85 percent may look acceptable until leaders see that one high-volume region is at 45 percent, night-shift employees were excluded from practice sessions, or a critical role group did not receive job-specific guidance. Governance should look for uneven adoption because uneven adoption is where customer problems, compliance gaps, and informal workarounds often begin.

The team should define decision thresholds in advance where possible. For example, launch may require 95 percent completion for legally required training, named owners for all high-severity issues, confirmed manager readiness in every impacted function, and a tested support path for day-one questions. Thresholds do not remove judgment, but they reduce improvisation. When leaders override a threshold, the decision should be explicit and documented, including the risk being accepted.

### Section Summary
- Governance decisions need multiple forms of evidence, not one favored metric.
- Dashboards should support judgment by showing readiness, risk, decisions, and variation.
- Predefined thresholds improve launch discipline and make risk acceptance explicit.

- W. Edwards Deming, management theory on systems, variation, and measurement.

## Avoiding Reporting Theater

Governance becomes theater when meetings exist mainly to confirm that the plan is still green. Teams then spend more time packaging status than solving problems. The meeting may look disciplined, but the work underneath becomes less honest. People learn to smooth bad news, delay escalation, and translate uncertainty into optimistic language. The result is a governance system that protects the appearance of control while weakening actual control.

The first warning sign is the absence of meaningful decisions. If a governance meeting runs for several cycles without making decisions, changing priorities, removing barriers, or redirecting effort, it may be a reporting meeting rather than a governance meeting. Reporting has value, but it should not be confused with leadership. Leaders govern when they make choices, allocate attention, resolve conflict, and accept or reject risk.

The second warning sign is excessive status production. When teams spend days preparing slides for a one-hour meeting, the governance cost may exceed the governance value. A leaner approach is to standardize the minimum status pack and keep it stable. The pack should include a concise view of progress, risks, decisions, adoption evidence, and actions since the last meeting. Custom analysis should be reserved for real decisions.

The third warning sign is persistent green status with visible field friction. Employees may be confused, managers may be improvising, support teams may be overloaded, and customers may be experiencing inconsistent service, while the program dashboard remains green because build milestones are on schedule. This disconnect usually means the governance system is measuring delivery activity more than implementation health. The cure is to add field evidence and adoption indicators to the core review.

A better governance meeting focuses on decisions needed, risks requiring intervention, learning from the field, and tradeoffs that require leadership. The meeting should be short enough to maintain discipline and frequent enough to keep issues from aging. Pre-reads can carry information. Meeting time should carry judgment. The chair should ask, "What decision do we need from this group?" and "What will remain blocked if we do not act today?"

### Section Summary
- Reporting theater creates the appearance of control while discouraging honest escalation.
- Governance meetings should produce decisions, barrier removal, risk acceptance, or clear next actions.
- Field friction should be visible in governance even when delivery milestones are on track.

## Managing Scope, Exceptions, and Tradeoffs

Implementation rarely follows the original plan exactly. Teams discover process gaps, local constraints, data problems, customer commitments, policy conflicts, and system limitations. Governance must distinguish between useful adaptation and uncontrolled scope drift. Useful adaptation improves fit while preserving the intent of the change. Scope drift expands the work, delays benefits, and often creates complexity that the organization cannot sustain.

Scope decisions should be made against explicit criteria:

- Does the request protect legal, safety, customer, or financial obligations?
- Does it improve adoption for a critical user group?
- Does it preserve the value case?
- Does it create complexity for another team?
- Does it require additional funding, training, system design, or support capacity?
- Does it change the promised benefits or timing?

These questions move the conversation from preference to consequence.

Exceptions require equal discipline. Some exceptions are necessary because a location, customer segment, union agreement, regulatory requirement, or operational constraint truly differs from the standard design. Other exceptions are resistance in procedural form. A leader may request an exception because their team is busy, because the old process is comfortable, or because local metrics reward different behavior. Governance should not reject all exceptions. It should require evidence, define the duration, identify the owner, and specify how the exception will be reviewed.

Tradeoffs should be named rather than hidden. A launch date may be protected by reducing scope. Scope may be protected by adding resources or extending timeline. Adoption quality may be protected by delaying launch for a critical group. Cost may be protected by accepting more manual work during transition. None of these choices is automatically right. They become dangerous when leaders act as if no tradeoff exists.

A practical tool is the tradeoff brief. It should be one page and include the decision required, options considered, recommendation, benefits, risks, affected stakeholders, cost or schedule impact, adoption impact, and consequence of deferral. This format helps leaders make decisions without forcing the team to produce lengthy narratives. It also creates a record that future teams can understand.

### Section Summary
- Governance must separate useful adaptation from uncontrolled scope drift.
- Exceptions should have evidence, owners, duration, and review points.
- Tradeoffs should be made explicit so leaders understand what they are accepting.

## Leader Roles in Implementation Governance

Leaders govern implementation through more than attendance. Their role is to set priorities, make decisions, remove barriers, reinforce expectations, and model the seriousness of the change. A sponsor who only opens meetings with general support is not providing enough governance. Sponsorship becomes operational when leaders use their authority to resolve conflicts that the project team cannot resolve alone.

The executive sponsor should own strategic alignment and major tradeoffs. This includes confirming that the change remains connected to business priorities, resolving conflicts among senior leaders, approving material scope changes, and accepting significant risk. The sponsor should also ensure that the leadership team gives consistent signals. If one executive treats the change as required and another treats it as optional, employees will notice the inconsistency and follow the signal that best fits local pressure.

Process owners should own the design of the work. They decide how the process should operate, what standards matter, which variations are allowed, and how quality will be monitored. Other leadership roles should be equally explicit:

- Technology owners own system integrity, configuration choices, data requirements, and support readiness.
- People leaders own local readiness, manager routines, role clarity, and reinforcement.
- Change practitioners facilitate readiness, stakeholder engagement, communication, training alignment, and adoption diagnostics.

Change practitioners should not become substitutes for accountable business leaders.

Managers have a specific governance role that is often underestimated. They translate decisions into daily expectations. They identify friction before it reaches dashboards. They notice when employees use workarounds, misunderstand the process, or receive conflicting instructions. Governance should give managers a path to raise issues and receive answers. If managers are only recipients of decisions, the governance system loses its closest observers of adoption.

Leader behavior should be part of the governance review. Are leaders attending the forums where their decisions are required? Are they communicating consistently? Are they resolving issues within agreed time frames? Are they reinforcing the change in their own operating routines? A governance system that reviews employee adoption but ignores leader follow-through is incomplete.

### Section Summary
- Leaders govern by making decisions, removing barriers, and reinforcing expectations.
- Sponsors, process owners, technology owners, people leaders, managers, and change practitioners have different roles.
- Manager feedback is a critical source of implementation intelligence.

## Governance Checklists and Diagnostics

Checklists are useful when they prevent avoidable misses. They should not replace judgment, but they can keep governance from overlooking common failure points. A governance checklist for implementation should cover decision rights, risk visibility, adoption evidence, stakeholder alignment, readiness, support capacity, communication, training, issue aging, and handoff. The checklist should be reviewed at major gates and during high-risk periods.

A launch readiness checklist should ask whether the affected groups know what is changing, why it matters, what they must do differently, where they can get help, and how success will be measured. It should also ask whether managers can explain the change, coach the new behavior, handle resistance, and escalate issues. Technical readiness matters, but implementation readiness requires the people side to be equally visible.

A decision quality checklist should ask whether the decision owner is clear, whether the right stakeholders were consulted, whether the evidence is sufficient, whether options were considered, whether downstream impacts are known, and whether the decision has been communicated. Many weak decisions are not obviously wrong at the time. They are weak because the process omitted key information or affected groups.

A failure mode diagnostic can help leaders intervene before problems harden. Common failure modes include slow decisions, unclear authority, hidden adoption risk, over-customization, unmanaged exceptions, competing metrics, overloaded managers, weak support paths, and dashboards that average away local problems. For each failure mode, governance should identify the earliest visible signal and the corrective action. For example, if support tickets show repeated questions about the same process step, the response may be a process clarification, manager talking points, or a training update rather than another general communication.

The most useful diagnostic question is, "What would make the new way unreasonable for a capable employee trying to do good work?" This question shifts governance from blaming people to examining conditions. Employees may resist because they dislike change, but they may also resist because the workflow is slower, the policy conflicts with customer needs, the system is unreliable, or managers are measured on old expectations. Governance should be strong enough to tell the difference.

### Section Summary
- Checklists help governance teams catch predictable gaps without replacing judgment.
- Launch readiness should include manager capability, support paths, and adoption evidence.
- Failure mode diagnostics help leaders address conditions rather than blame behavior.

## Conclusion

Governance gives change a decision-making spine. When it is practical and evidence based, it helps leaders remove barriers before those barriers become reasons for employees to return to old ways. It also protects the organization from treating implementation as a sequence of status updates rather than a series of decisions under uncertainty.

The strongest governance systems are clear, lean, and honest. They define decision rights, use evidence from the field, escalate issues early, manage tradeoffs explicitly, and hold leaders accountable for the conditions required for adoption. They do not eliminate surprises. They create a disciplined way to respond to surprises before they damage value.

## Chapter Summary
- Governance exists to improve decisions, clarify authority, and remove barriers.
- Decision rights and decision logs protect momentum and organizational memory.
- Adoption risk belongs in implementation governance alongside schedule, cost, technical, compliance, and operational risk.
- Governance forums should have distinct purposes, appropriate cadence, and decision-focused agendas.
- Scope changes, exceptions, and tradeoffs require explicit criteria and documented ownership.
- Leaders, managers, process owners, technology owners, and change practitioners each have specific governance responsibilities.
- Checklists and diagnostics help teams identify weak signals before they become adoption failures.
