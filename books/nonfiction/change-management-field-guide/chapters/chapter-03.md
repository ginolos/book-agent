# Chapter 3: Map the Real System

Organizations often plan change around formal charts, approved process diagrams, and project plans. Those artifacts matter, but they rarely describe how work is actually accomplished. People experience change through the real system of work: the process steps that are documented, the shortcuts that are not, the approvals that matter in practice, the data that arrives late or incomplete, the tools people trust, the tools they avoid, the incentives that shape behavior, the habits that keep the operation moving, and the informal relationships that help people solve problems when the official path is too slow.

If a change team maps only the official process, it will miss the places where adoption will struggle. A policy can be clear and still conflict with local targets. A technology can be well configured and still require people to enter information they do not have. A new operating model can look logical in a workshop and still fail because managers do not have time to coach their teams through the transition. System mapping brings these conditions into view before the organization commits to a plan that assumes too much.

Mapping the real system is not an academic exercise. It is a practical discipline for reducing avoidable failure. It helps leaders see who is affected, what must change, where the plan is too optimistic, and which adoption risks are being hidden by polite agreement. This is especially important when a technical project is expected to produce a human outcome. Most change initiatives do not fail because the organization lacks slides, slogans, or urgency. They fail because the change design does not fit the operating environment people actually inhabit.

## Impact Mapping

An impact map identifies the groups affected by the change and describes what will be different for each group. A useful map goes beyond job titles and departments. It includes tasks, decisions, tools, handoffs, metrics, policies, relationships, timing, information needs, and exceptions. It should show whether a group must learn something new, stop doing something familiar, accept a loss of control, take on new accountability, or depend on another group in a different way.

The discipline is to make impact specific enough that it can be managed. "Finance is impacted" is too broad to guide action. "Finance analysts must review expense coding in the new platform by noon each business day, using a new exception queue, while managers remain accountable for coding accuracy" is actionable. It points to training, workflow design, decision rights, reporting, support needs, and potential resistance. The map should clarify what is changing, who must change behavior, what good performance looks like, and what conditions must be present for that performance to occur.

For example, a procurement platform change may appear to affect only buyers and approvers. A deeper map may reveal impacts on finance coding, supplier onboarding, legal review, budget ownership, reporting, field teams that make urgent purchases, and executives who monitor committed spend. Each hidden impact creates a readiness requirement. Field teams may need a mobile purchasing path. Legal may need new thresholds for contract review. Finance may need redesigned account coding controls. Managers may need clearer rules for emergency exceptions. Without impact mapping, these requirements often surface after go-live as complaints, workaround requests, or data quality problems.

A practical impact map can be built with a simple structure. For each stakeholder group, capture the fields that turn a broad impact into something the team can act on:

- Current behavior
- Future behavior
- Difference between the two
- Level of disruption
- Operational dependency
- Readiness requirement
- Owner
- Loss or concern

The final field matters because many change impacts are not only procedural. People may lose autonomy, status, speed, familiar expertise, direct access to decision makers, or confidence in how to succeed. Naming these losses does not mean every concern should govern the design. It means the change team is treating adoption as a real operating problem, not as a communication problem.

The map should also distinguish direct, indirect, and cumulative impacts. Direct impacts change a group's own work. Indirect impacts change what the group receives from others or what others expect from it. Cumulative impacts occur when several initiatives land on the same people at the same time. A customer service team may be able to absorb a new knowledge base, a new quality scorecard, and a new scheduling policy separately. Together, those changes may overwhelm attention and create performance dips that leaders wrongly attribute to resistance. Impact mapping should therefore include other active changes, seasonal workload, staffing constraints, and known pain points.

A useful diagnostic question is: "What would make this change harder in the real workday than it looks in the plan?" Common answers include:

- Unclear decision rights
- Duplicate data entry
- Conflicting measures
- Insufficient manager capacity
- Missing job aids
- Exceptions that the system cannot handle
- Incentives that reward the old behavior

These answers should become design inputs. If the map identifies a high-disruption group with low support and unclear ownership, the project has found an adoption risk, not a stakeholder to be "managed" later.

### Section Summary
- Impact mapping turns broad stakeholder labels into specific adoption requirements.
- The map should include behavioral, procedural, data, tool, policy, timing, and relationship changes.
- Hidden impacts often appear after go-live as workarounds, complaints, data issues, or performance dips.
- Losses and concerns should be documented because they affect readiness, even when they do not change the final decision.

## Formal and Informal Networks

Formal hierarchy explains authority, but informal networks explain how information, trust, and interpretation move. The organization chart shows who can approve budget, assign work, or set policy. It does not show who employees ask when a system behaves unexpectedly, whose judgment managers trust before endorsing a change, or which long-tenured employee can explain why an old workaround exists. These informal networks are part of the real system. Ignoring them leaves the change team dependent on formal announcements that may reach people without persuading them.

Informal influence is not the same as popularity. Some influential employees are connectors who move information across groups. Some are experts whose technical or operational judgment carries weight. Some are sensemakers who help peers interpret what leadership decisions mean. Some are blockers because they can make a change appear impractical through skepticism, silence, or selective compliance. The point of mapping these roles is not to manipulate people. The point is to understand how the organization actually learns, tests credibility, and decides whether a change deserves effort.

Mapping informal influence should be respectful and specific. A change team can ask managers, frontline employees, and cross-functional partners questions such as: "Who do people go to when this process breaks?" "Who understands the exceptions?" "Whose opinion would cause others to take this seriously?" "Where does information slow down or get distorted?" "Which groups have low trust because of previous changes?" These questions should be used to improve involvement and design quality, not to create a list of targets. Employees should never feel that the organization is surveilling relationships in order to neutralize dissent.

A practical network map can identify three categories of participation. First, include formal decision makers who can allocate resources and remove barriers. Second, include operational experts who can test whether the solution fits real work. Third, include trusted representatives who can surface concerns early and explain what has been validated. These groups do not have identical roles. Decision makers should not dominate design details. Operational experts should not be expected to carry executive sponsorship. Trusted representatives should not be asked to sell a change they believe is flawed. Clarity about role prevents misuse of informal influence.

Consider a claims processing department implementing new automation. The official sponsor is the operations vice president, and the project team reports readiness through a steering committee. Yet supervisors privately wait to hear from two senior claims specialists before changing team routines, because those specialists know the edge cases. If those specialists are excluded until training, they may discover problems late and become skeptical. If they are involved during design and testing, they can identify exception paths, improve job aids, and tell peers which concerns were addressed. Their influence becomes a channel for operational truth rather than a late source of resistance.

Failure modes are common. One is relying only on managers for feedback, which can filter out frontline friction. Another is selecting "change champions" based on enthusiasm rather than credibility. A third is treating dissent as a communication defect instead of evidence that the design may be incomplete. A fourth is overusing trusted employees by asking them to attend workshops, test tools, train peers, answer questions, and absorb frustration without adjusting their workload. Informal influence is a resource, but it is not free capacity. If the change depends on these people, their time should be planned and protected.

### Section Summary
- Formal authority and informal influence both shape adoption.
- Informal networks reveal how people test credibility, solve problems, and interpret leadership intent.
- Trusted operational voices should be included early enough to improve the design, not only to promote it.
- Change teams should protect influential employees from being overloaded with unpaid change work.

## Separating Technical and Adoption Work

Technical work asks whether the solution can be built, configured, launched, integrated, secured, or enforced. Adoption work asks whether people can and will use the solution correctly in context. These workstreams overlap, but they require different questions, owners, measures, and evidence. A technically ready system can still fail if people do not understand when to use it, if managers reinforce the old behavior, if the data entry burden is unrealistic, or if exception handling remains unclear.

The distinction matters because many organizations use technical progress as a proxy for change readiness. A project plan may show data migration, configuration, testing, deployment, and defect closure. Those milestones are necessary, but they do not prove that the organization is ready to behave differently. The adoption plan should show stakeholder readiness, manager enablement, role-based training, support channels, reinforcement, communications, process ownership, measurement, and post-launch stabilization. When both plans are visible, leaders can see whether go-live readiness is genuine or merely technical.

A simple way to separate the work is to ask two columns of questions.

| Readiness lens | Questions to answer |
| --- | --- |
| Technical readiness | Does the system work? Are integrations complete? Has security been tested? Are defects within tolerance? Is data migrated? Are service levels defined? |
| Adoption readiness | Who must change behavior? Do they know what is changing and why? Have managers practiced the conversations they need to lead? Can users complete real scenarios without project team assistance? Are incentives aligned? Is support staffed for the first weeks after launch? Will leaders know whether the new behavior is happening? |

This distinction should appear in governance. The steering committee should not accept a green status based only on build completion. A launch decision should include adoption evidence: completion of role-based practice, manager readiness, critical process walkthroughs, support readiness, communications delivered and understood, high-risk stakeholder issues resolved or owned, and baseline measures established. The evidence does not need to be elaborate, but it must be concrete. "Training deck complete" is weaker than "ninety percent of affected users completed scenario-based practice, and the remaining users have scheduled sessions before launch."

Adoption work also needs owners who have authority in the business, not only members of the project team. A system integrator can configure workflows and produce training material, but it cannot own whether supervisors coach the new behavior or whether executives stop accepting reports from the old tool. Business ownership is especially important for reinforcement. If leaders continue asking for the old spreadsheet because it is familiar, employees will conclude that the new system is optional. If performance metrics still reward speed over data quality, people will optimize for speed and create cleanup work downstream.

The clearest failure mode is a "go-live cliff." Before launch, the project team carries knowledge, answers questions, manually resolves issues, and shields business teams from ambiguity. After launch, the team reduces support and assumes the operating organization will absorb the new way of working. If adoption work has been underbuilt, the cliff appears as low usage, parallel processes, support tickets, manager frustration, and leadership disappointment. A stabilization plan reduces this risk by defining hypercare coverage, escalation paths, daily issue review, decision turnaround expectations, and criteria for moving from project support to business-as-usual ownership.

### Section Summary
- Technical readiness and adoption readiness are related but distinct.
- Green technical status does not prove that people are ready to work in a new way.
- Launch governance should require evidence of role-based readiness, manager enablement, support capacity, and reinforcement.
- Business leaders must own behavior change that cannot be delegated to a project team or vendor.

## Mapping Workarounds, Exceptions, and Friction

Workarounds are often treated as bad behavior, but they usually contain useful information. People create workarounds when the official process is too slow, incomplete, unclear, or misaligned with local demands. Some workarounds are risky and should be eliminated. Others keep customers served despite broken upstream conditions. Before changing a process or system, the team should understand what each workaround accomplishes, what risk it creates, and what condition caused it to appear.

The most important question is not "How do we stop people from doing this?" It is "What job is this workaround doing for the organization?" A sales team may maintain a shadow forecast because the official system cannot capture deal nuance. A warehouse may bypass a scanning step because labels are unreadable in certain conditions. A customer service team may use a private knowledge document because the approved knowledge base is outdated. Removing these practices without addressing the underlying need will not create compliance. It will create hidden work, slower service, or quiet reversion.

Exception mapping should identify the normal path and the conditions that make the path harder to follow:

- Known exception types
- Decision rights
- Frequency
- Risk
- Current handling
- Future handling
- Unresolved design questions

This is especially important for regulated environments, high-volume operations, and customer-facing work. A process that handles eighty percent of cases but fails on the twenty percent that consume most attention will be experienced as a poor process. The design must account for the difficult cases, not only the clean ones used in demonstrations.

Friction should be measured from the user's point of view. Count handoffs, screens, approvals, duplicate entries, waiting points, unclear decisions, rework loops, and moments where people must leave one tool to complete work in another. Also look for emotional friction: fear of making a visible mistake, loss of confidence, concern about being measured unfairly, or frustration from being asked to follow a process that leaders bypass. These conditions affect adoption because they shape whether the new behavior feels possible and legitimate.

A practical diagnostic is the "day-in-the-life test." Select representative roles and walk through real scenarios from start to finish, including exceptions, interruptions, incomplete information, peak workload, and manager questions. Ask participants to narrate what they would do, what they would worry about, what they would delay, and where they would use a workaround. The test should occur before final design decisions when possible. If it occurs only after the solution is built, it becomes a defect-finding exercise rather than a design input.

The change team should classify findings into four categories. Design changes are issues the solution or process should fix before launch. Enablement needs are issues that require training, job aids, manager guidance, or practice. Governance decisions are issues that require policy, authority, funding, or prioritization. Accepted risks are issues the organization chooses not to solve now, with a named owner and monitoring plan. This classification keeps the map practical. It prevents the team from documenting friction without deciding what to do about it.

### Section Summary
- Workarounds reveal unmet needs, broken conditions, or local constraints.
- Exception mapping prevents the design from working only for clean cases.
- Friction includes procedural burden and the emotional cost of uncertainty, exposure, or unfair measurement.
- Findings should be converted into design changes, enablement needs, governance decisions, or accepted risks.

## Building the Real System Map

A real system map should be detailed enough to guide decisions but simple enough to keep current. It is usually a combination of artifacts rather than a single diagram. The core set often includes a stakeholder impact map, process or value stream map, informal network view, decision-rights table, risk register, readiness assessment, and stabilization plan. The format matters less than the discipline of connecting these views. A risk in the impact map should connect to an owner in the readiness plan. A workaround in the process map should connect to a design decision or accepted risk. A dependency in the network map should connect to communication and involvement planning.

The mapping process should use multiple sources. Workshops are useful for structure, but they often privilege people with status or confidence. Interviews help reveal concerns that people may not raise in a group. Observation shows differences between documented process and actual behavior. Data can identify bottlenecks, error rates, rework, usage patterns, and seasonal variation. Support tickets and audit findings can show where the current system already fails. The strongest maps combine these sources instead of treating any one source as complete.

A practical sequence is to start with scope, then expand into reality. First, define the change in plain operational terms: what is being introduced, removed, replaced, centralized, automated, standardized, or measured differently. Second, identify affected roles and workflows. Third, map current work, including exceptions and workarounds. Fourth, define future work and compare the two. Fifth, identify adoption requirements by role. Sixth, assign owners and evidence for readiness. Seventh, review the map with people who do the work and revise it based on what they challenge.

The review step is important because maps can create false confidence. A polished diagram may look authoritative even when it is based on assumptions. The team should actively test the map with questions such as:

- Which part of this would fail on a busy day?
- Which handoff is most likely to be skipped?
- Where will people keep a spreadsheet?
- What decision will managers avoid making?
- Which group is shown as receiving work but not given capacity?
- What would customers notice first if this design is wrong?

These questions turn the map from documentation into inquiry.

Ownership should be visible. Every material adoption requirement needs an accountable owner, a due date, and evidence of completion. If the requirement is "supervisors can coach the new quality review process," evidence might include supervisor briefing, practice with scenarios, talking points, escalation rules, and observation during the first week after launch. If the requirement is "field teams can submit urgent purchases without blocking operations," evidence might include mobile testing, emergency approval rules, supplier coverage, and support availability during local operating hours.

The map should also be treated as a living artifact. Conditions change during a project. Leaders adjust scope, vendors miss dates, teams reorganize, policies shift, and other initiatives compete for attention. A map created early and never revisited becomes another version of the imaginary organization. Review the map at major design decisions, before training, before launch readiness review, and during stabilization. Each review should ask whether the real system has changed and whether the adoption plan still matches it.

### Section Summary
- A real system map is usually a connected set of artifacts, not one master diagram.
- Workshops, interviews, observation, operational data, support tickets, and audit findings all provide useful evidence.
- The map should be tested with people who do the work and challenged with realistic scenarios.
- Adoption requirements need named owners, due dates, and observable evidence of readiness.

## Leadership Use of the Map

The real system map has value only if leaders use it to make decisions. If it becomes a project appendix, it will not change outcomes. Leaders should use the map to adjust scope, sequence work, allocate capacity, resolve policy conflicts, protect critical roles, and decide whether launch timing is credible. The map gives leaders a more grounded view of change demand. It shows not only what the organization wants to implement, but what the organization is being asked to absorb.

One leadership use is sequencing. If a map shows that one role is hit by several high-disruption changes at once, leaders can decide whether to delay one initiative, reduce scope, increase support, or accept a defined performance risk. Another use is prioritization. If a map shows that adoption depends on resolving decision rights between two departments, leaders can address that conflict before it becomes a launch issue. A third use is capacity management. If supervisors are expected to coach new behavior, attend training, manage productivity dips, and handle escalations, leaders may need to reduce other demands temporarily.

The map also improves sponsor behavior. Sponsors often communicate the strategic reason for change, but they may not know where the operating model creates friction. A good map lets sponsors speak more concretely. Instead of saying, "We know change is hard," a sponsor can say, "We have heard that the urgent purchase path, supplier setup timing, and duplicate coding requirements are the three biggest concerns. Here is what will change before launch, and here is what we will monitor afterward." That kind of specificity increases credibility because it shows that leadership has listened at the level of work.

Leaders should resist using the map as a reason to wait until all uncertainty is gone. The goal is not perfect knowledge. The goal is informed action. Some risks will remain, and some impacts will be discovered only after launch. The difference is whether those risks are visible, owned, and monitored. A mature launch decision can acknowledge open issues while explaining why the organization is ready enough, what support will be in place, and what criteria would trigger intervention.

The map can also prevent symbolic change management. Symbolic change management focuses on messages, slogans, town halls, and training attendance while avoiding harder questions about incentives, workload, power, and process design. Leaders may prefer symbolic activity because it is visible and less uncomfortable. The real system map brings the conversation back to operating conditions. If incentives reward the old behavior, if managers are not prepared, if the process fails on common exceptions, or if support cannot handle expected volume, more communication will not solve the problem.

A leadership checklist should be brief and direct. Before approving launch, leaders should ask:

- Are the highest-impact roles identified?
- Are the biggest behavior changes clear?
- Are managers ready to reinforce the new behavior?
- Are critical exceptions designed or governed?
- Are incentives and measures aligned?
- Are support channels staffed?
- Are known risks owned?
- Do we know how adoption will be measured after launch?

If the answer to any question is weak, the decision is not automatically to delay. The decision is to assign ownership, adjust the plan, or consciously accept the risk.

### Section Summary
- Leaders should use the map for scope, sequencing, capacity, conflict resolution, and launch decisions.
- Sponsor communication becomes more credible when it addresses specific operational concerns.
- The map should support informed action, not endless analysis.
- Communication and training cannot compensate for unresolved incentives, decision rights, workload, or process design issues.

## Conclusion

Mapping the real system prevents the change team from designing for an imaginary organization. It reveals the practical conditions that must be addressed before people can adopt the new way with confidence. The work requires humility because the formal process is rarely the whole truth. It also requires discipline because every discovered impact, workaround, exception, and network relationship cannot become an excuse for delay. The value is in converting messy reality into decisions, ownership, and readiness evidence.

The most useful maps are grounded in actual work. They show who must behave differently, which conditions make that behavior hard, which informal networks shape trust, which technical milestones do not prove adoption, and which leaders must resolve conflicts before launch. They also remain alive after the first workshop. As the project moves from design to deployment to stabilization, the map should keep testing whether the plan still fits the organization people experience every day.

### Section Summary
- Real work includes formal process, informal habits, incentives, tools, exceptions, constraints, and relationships.
- Impact mapping turns abstract change into specific adoption requirements.
- Technical and adoption readiness should be managed as related but distinct work.
- The real system map is most valuable when leaders use it to make concrete decisions.
