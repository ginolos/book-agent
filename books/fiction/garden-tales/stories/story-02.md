# Robin Pip and the Bluebell Bell

After a windy afternoon, the apple tree looked as though it had been laughing too hard. Leaves were scattered across the grass, one small twig had landed in the mint bed, and a ribbon of pale yellow grass lay looped over a branch where it did not belong.

Pip the robin hopped from branch to branch, peering at the neat little home he shared with his family. Tucked safely among the leaves was their nest, and tied to the smallest side branch was a blue ribbon. Pip's mother had tied it there in spring so the youngest chicks would know which branch was theirs when they began practicing tiny hops.

But this evening the ribbon was gone.

"Maybe the wind borrowed it," said Pip's sister, tilting her head.

Pip tried to laugh, but he felt a fluttery worry in his chest. The littlest branch looked like many others now. In the deepening dusk, he was afraid the youngest chick might hop the wrong way in the morning.

"I will find it," he said quickly. "I can do it alone."

He flew down to the grass and searched beneath the daisies. He inspected the thyme patch. He even looked inside an upside-down flowerpot where Pebble the toad sometimes rested. No ribbon.

As he searched, he heard a faint ding.

Pip turned. Hanging from a bent bluebell blossom was a droplet so round and bright that it chimed softly whenever the stem swayed. Ding. Ding.

The sound made Pip think of home, of the branch that held his family's nest steady in every breeze.

Still, he kept searching by himself. He peered under leaves and behind the watering can. He examined a curl of onion skin, a strip of bark, and one very suspicious earthworm that turned out to be only an earthworm.

At last he landed on a low fence and let out a tired sigh.

His mother fluttered down beside him. "You are searching with your eyes so hard that you have forgotten your ears."

"My ears?" said Pip.

"A home has sounds," she replied. "Listen for what belongs to you."

Pip felt a little embarrassed. He had wanted to be useful all on his own. But the garden was not a place where anyone truly lived alone. Even the breeze seemed to travel with the leaves.

So he nodded and listened.

The mint bed made a soft hiss. The bean poles creaked. A ladybug clicked its tiny feet on a stone. Then came the gentle ding of the bluebell bell again, this time from near the old wheelbarrow.

Pip followed the sound. There, caught on the handle, was the missing blue ribbon, its end tied around the stem of the bluebell as if the wind had tried to make a little bell of its own.

"There you are," Pip whispered.

He tugged the ribbon free and almost took off at once, but the bluebell swayed sadly without it. Pip paused, then carefully tied a loose grass blade around the stem so the dewdrop could still ring when the breeze blew.

Ding.

It sounded pleased.

When Pip returned to the apple tree, his family welcomed him with cheerful chirps. Together they tied the ribbon back on the smallest branch, making the knot snug and neat.

"You found it," said his sister.

Pip nodded. "I found it after I listened."

The youngest chick peeped sleepily from the nest. "Can I hear the ribbon branch?"

So Pip hopped onto it and made it tremble gently. The leaves rustled in a sound the little chick knew at once.

"That one," the chick said happily.

Pip settled beside his family as dusk deepened around the apple tree. Somewhere below, the bluebell bell gave one last tiny chime in the breeze.

He understood then that asking for help did not make him less clever or less brave. It simply helped him hear what mattered sooner.

The branch with the ribbon swayed softly above him, and Pip tucked his head beneath his wing, feeling the tree, the sounds, and his family all around him like a safe and familiar song.
