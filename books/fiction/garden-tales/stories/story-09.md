# Toad Pebble and the Lily Pad Bells

Pebble the toad liked evenings by the pond. The water held the last color of the sky, and the lily pads rocked so gently that one could almost believe the pond was singing itself to sleep.

On some nights, if a droplet landed just right on the edge of a pad, it made a tiny ringing note.

Pebble called them lily pad bells.

Tonight he was hoping for a chorus, because a cluster of young tadpoles had recently arrived in the shallow end of the pond. They were no longer tiny enough to hide under every leaf, yet they still did exactly that whenever anyone invited them to join the garden's evening song.

"Our voices are too little," one tadpole had whispered.

"Too watery," said another.

"Too new," said a third.

Pebble understood shyness. A small voice could feel very far away from belonging.

So he sat on his favorite smooth stone and began with a hum so low and warm it almost sounded like the pond itself. Hmmmm.

The lily pads answered with the gentlest ring.

Pebble hummed again, this time with a tiny pause in the middle, leaving a quiet space where another sound could fit if it wished. The reeds swayed. The water made a round little lap against the shore.

From beneath a lily pad came the faintest peep.

Pebble did not look directly. He simply nodded as if that peep had always belonged.

"A very fine pond note," he said.

Another peep joined it. Then one more.

Soon the shy tadpoles were peeking from the shadows, their eyes bright and uncertain. Pebble kept the tune simple. Hum. Pause. Ring. Hum. Pause. Peep.

The song did not ask anyone to be loud. It only made room.

One bold tadpole paddled closer. "Will it spoil the song if we sound different?"

Pebble smiled. "If the pond tried to sound only like a toad, it would be a very dull pond indeed."

That made the tadpoles wiggle with laughter. One by one they added their own tiny watery notes. They were high where Pebble was deep, quick where he was slow, but together they formed a tune that shimmered like ripples under moonlight.

The lily pad bells rang softly whenever the breeze leaned over the pond.

When the song ended, the tadpoles stayed near the stone instead of hiding at once.

"I thought belonging meant sounding the same," one admitted.

"No," said Pebble. "Belonging means there is a place for your voice too."

The youngest tadpole let out an especially proud little peep, and everyone laughed in the gentle way friends do when joy is too full to stay quiet.

As the stars brightened overhead, the tadpoles drifted back to their sleeping places with happier hearts. Pebble remained on his stone a while longer, listening to the water settle.

He was pleased, not because he had sung the grandest song, but because he had helped a few small voices discover that they were welcome.

The last lily pad bell rang once in the dark.

To Pebble, it sounded exactly like yes.
