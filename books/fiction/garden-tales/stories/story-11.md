# Mouse Fennel and the Mixed-Up Seeds

The garden shed smelled of twine, dry wood, and paper seed packets. To Fennel the mouse, it was one of the most satisfying places in the world. Shelves lined the walls. Clay pots nested in tidy towers. A measuring tape lay curled in a drawer like a sleepy yellow snake.

Most important of all, it was almost time to organize the seeds for spring planting.

Fennel adored organizing.

He stacked the packets by shape and season. Beans with beans. Poppies with poppies. Nasturtiums in a cheerful orange row. He even lined up the labels so their corners matched.

Then, while carrying a small bundle from one shelf to another, he tripped over the curled measuring tape.

Packets fluttered everywhere.

"Oh no," Fennel squeaked.

He scrambled to gather them before anyone saw. Some had burst open slightly. Tiny seeds peppered the floorboards. Fennel did his best to sort them quickly, but the seeds looked confusingly alike. Were these parsley or carrot? Poppy or foxglove? He guessed, tucked them back into packets, and pressed the flaps closed.

No one would know, he told himself.

But the thought made his whiskers droop.

All afternoon he tried to ignore the uneasy feeling. When Clover asked whether the seeds were ready, Fennel said, "Nearly." When Sorrel peeked in, he smiled too brightly. By evening, his stomach felt like a knot of string.

At last he climbed onto the workbench and looked at the rows of packets. They were neat on the outside.

Inside, they might be all wrong.

If he kept quiet, the mistake would grow roots of its own. Flowers might bloom in the wrong place. Herbs might sprout where beans should be. The whole spring plan could wobble.

So when the others gathered near the shed to talk about planting, Fennel took a deep breath.

"I mixed up some of the seed packets," he admitted.

Nobody gasped. Nobody frowned.

Pebble only asked, "How mixed up?"

"Enough that guessing is a poor plan," said Fennel.

Tansy landed beside him on the bench. "Then we shall not guess."

Together they spread the packets out under a lantern glow. Sorrel recognized bean seeds by their shape. Marigold the bee remembered which envelopes had smelled faintly of sunflower dust. Moss patiently rolled the tiniest seeds into separate little groups. Fennel used the measuring tape, now behaving itself, to make neat rows while everyone compared notes.

Bit by bit, the muddle became order again.

By moonrise, every packet was labeled correctly.

Fennel let out such a long sigh that Clover laughed. "You look lighter."

"I am lighter," he said. "I thought telling the truth would make the trouble bigger."

"Truth tends to do the opposite," Pebble replied.

Fennel carefully placed the sorted packets back on the shelf. This time he tucked the measuring tape into a proper basket first.

When he curled into a nest of soft cloth scraps later that night, he felt warm and steady. The mistake had not vanished, but honesty had turned it into something fixable.

And that, Fennel thought sleepily, was much nicer than carrying a secret as heavy as a whole shelf of seeds.
