# Dryad Sorrel and the Root Lanterns

Beneath the old pear tree, the roots curved above the soil like the walls of a little room. Moss grew between them. Fallen leaves made soft doorways. When evening came, amber lights glowed under the earth where Sorrel the dryad walked, and the roots shone like lantern windows in a hidden house.

Sorrel cared for the seedlings that grew in the pear tree's shade. Some needed more water. Some needed loose soil. Some wanted extra warmth from old leaves gathered around their stems. Sorrel usually knew just what to do.

One dry afternoon, however, she found three seedlings drooping at once.

"Oh, little leaves," she said, kneeling beside them. "Do not worry. I shall fix everything."

She fetched water in acorn caps and poured a little on each. Then she tucked leaves around each stem. Then she loosened the soil around each root. Yet still, one seedling drooped harder, another perked only slightly, and the third bent away from the others as if unhappy.

Sorrel hurried from one to the next. More water here. More mulch there. A shade leaf for all of them. Her bark-brown hands moved quickly, but the seedlings did not seem to improve.

At last she sat back, troubled.

From a nearby root, Rowan the seed sprite popped up, brushing dirt from her green hair.

"You are helping them all the same way," Rowan said gently.

"Shouldn't kindness be shared evenly?" Sorrel asked.

"Kindness, yes," Rowan replied. "Care, perhaps not."

Sorrel looked at the seedlings more closely.

The first one's soil was dry and crumbly. The second one's leaves were nibbled by sun and wanted shade. The third's roots were crowded against a small stone hidden below the surface.

How had she missed that?

Because, she realized, she had been listening to her worry more than to the plants.

So she slowed down.

To the first seedling she gave a deeper drink of water. Over the second she angled a broad fallen pear leaf to soften the late light. Around the third she dug carefully until she found the buried pebble and moved it aside, freeing the roots to spread.

Then she waited.

The root lanterns glowed warmly under the ground as evening approached. In their amber light, the first seedling lifted a little. The second stopped shrinking from the sun. The third relaxed toward the open soil.

Sorrel smiled with relief.

Each had needed help, but not the same help.

Rowan sat beside her on a root. "You heard them better that time."

"I did," Sorrel said. "I thought caring meant rushing to do many things. Perhaps caring begins by noticing what is truly needed."

The seed sprite nodded, pleased.

That night Sorrel walked among the roots while the lantern glow followed her steps. She checked each seedling again, not with anxious hurry, but with patient eyes. The little plants looked steadier now, as if they trusted that someone was finally listening in the right way.

When Sorrel rested beneath the trunk at last, she leaned her head against the bark and listened to the tree itself, old and slow and wise.

The garden, she thought, was always speaking.

The gentlest caretakers were the ones who remembered to hear it.
