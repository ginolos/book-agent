# Bee Marigold and the Sunflower Rest

Marigold the bee loved useful days. She loved them so much that she sometimes forgot useful days needed pauses tucked inside them, the way flowers held nectar inside petals.

This particular afternoon, the sunflower row was bright as polished gold. Pollen dust shimmered in the air. Every bloom seemed to call, "Here first. No, here."

Marigold buzzed from flower to flower with determined cheer.

"Such fine work," she said to herself.

By the fourth sunflower she was still cheerful. By the seventh she was less cheerful and more determined. By the tenth her wings had begun to ache.

Still she did not stop.

If I rest, she thought, I will fall behind.

Below, Clover nibbled clover blossoms in the shade. "You are flying crooked," she called up.

"I am flying efficiently," Marigold answered, though she bumped lightly into a petal at that very moment.

She carried on to the next bloom, but the world felt brighter and hotter than before. The rich smell of pollen made her head swim. When she landed, her legs trembled.

A soft breeze moved through the row. One broad sunflower leaf rocked beneath her like a gentle hammock.

"Just a moment," Marigold whispered to herself.

She stepped onto the leaf and folded her wings.

The shade was cool. The leaf dipped and swayed. Marigold had not realized how tired she was until stillness found her. She listened to the far-off hum of the garden, the rustle of stems, and the tiny ticking sound sunflower seeds made as they settled in their centers.

After a few quiet minutes, her breath felt easier. Her wings no longer ached so sharply.

From below, Clover looked up. "Better?"

Marigold stretched one wing, then the other. "Much."

She flew again, and to her surprise the next visit to a flower felt smooth and joyful instead of heavy. She gathered nectar carefully. She noticed a bloom she had missed before. She even had enough strength left to help a younger bee find the fullest sunflower in the row.

By late afternoon, Marigold had done good work after all.

Only now she understood something new: the rest on the leaf had not stolen time from her work. It had returned her best work to her.

When the sun lowered and the row grew softer in color, Marigold returned to the leaf for one final pause. Pollen dust still sparkled around her like drifting gold.

"Thank you," she said to the sunflower rest.

The leaf swayed as if it had heard.

That evening, Marigold tucked herself into her hive feeling pleasantly tired instead of thoroughly worn out. Tomorrow there would be more blossoms and more buzzing.

And somewhere between them, she knew, there would also be a place to pause.
