# Snail Moss and the Clover Thread

Moss the snail was small even for a garden snail. His shell was the color of warm brown tea, with a pale swirl in the center like cream stirred through it. When he moved across the stepping-stone path, he often heard other creatures say kind things such as, "Take your time," or, "We will see you tomorrow."

Moss did take his time. He could not help it.

One rainy evening, Tansy the fairy came to him with a tiny message rolled into a clover leaf and tied with a green thread.

"Would you deliver this to Sorrel the dryad beneath the pear tree?" she asked. "It is about the moonflowers. She needs to know before the petals close for the night."

Moss held the little leaf packet carefully. "I will do my very best."

He set off at once from the edge of the path. Raindrops clung to the stones, making them shine like mirrors. In each one Moss saw a tiny upside-down sky. The path was beautiful, but it was also slippery.

Soon Clover the rabbit bounded past. "Need a lift?" she asked kindly.

Moss almost said yes. Then he thought of the delicate message tied with clover thread. One big jump and it might blow away.

"Thank you," he said, "but I think I should carry it myself."

Clover twitched her nose. "Then I shall hop carefully around you."

She did.

Moss went on. A beetle scuttled by with much speed and importance. A robin flew overhead like a rusty arrow. Moss watched them all and felt a quiet sting of doubt.

If only I were faster, he thought, I would be more useful.

Just then he came to the broadest stone of the path. Rainwater had gathered in the middle, smooth as glass. On either side, muddy ground sloped down into little puddles. A fast creature might have dashed straight through. Moss paused.

He lowered his head and looked closely. Across the stone ran a thin dry line, protected by an arch of moss at the edge. It was narrow, but steady.

Slowly, carefully, he crossed there.

At the next stone he saw something else: a loose thorn that had fallen from a rose stem. If he had hurried, the message could have caught on it and torn. He edged around it and continued.

By the time he reached the pear tree, the sky was darkening and root lanterns glowed amber beneath the earth. Sorrel stepped from the trunk, her bark-brown hair braided with leaves.

"Moss," she said gently, "you have come through the rain."

He offered her the rolled clover leaf. "I hope I am not too late."

Sorrel opened it and smiled at once. "Not at all. This tells me to shade the moonflowers. A cold breeze is coming, and they would have closed too soon."

She bent to him with grateful eyes. "You arrived at just the right time."

Moss looked back along the wet path. He thought of the dry line across the stone, the thorn he had noticed, and the safe route he had chosen because he had been moving slowly enough to see them.

"I am not quick," he said, "but perhaps that is not always the same as being late."

Sorrel smiled. "The garden needs creatures who notice things."

She placed a tiny pear blossom beside him as a thank-you. Moss tucked it against his shell and began the slow journey home.

The raindrop mirrors still shone on the stepping stones, but now they seemed friendlier somehow, as if they were reflecting back a truer picture of him.

He was slow, yes.

And steady.

And tonight, that had been exactly enough.
