# Fairy Tansy and the Buttercup Lights

Behind the rosemary hedge, where the stems grew tall and woody and the air always smelled warm and green, there was a fairy circle no bigger than a tea tray. Smooth pebbles marked its edges. Thyme flowers made a soft purple carpet. In the middle stood a table woven from grass stems and lined with rose petals.

This was where Tansy the fairy planned to hold her glow-feast.

Friends were coming at dusk. Juniper the young unicorn would bring cool water from the birdbath. Moss the snail would carry sugared clover crumbs. Pip the robin had promised a bedtime tune. Tansy wanted everything to be perfect.

She lined up acorn cups for drinking. She folded mint leaves into tiny napkins. She tied floating buttercup lights to thread-fine strands of spider silk so they glowed gently above the table.

Then she stepped back and frowned.

The petals did not match.

Some were dark pink. Some were pale. One had a funny curl at the edge. Tansy moved them around. Then she moved them again. She swapped the acorn cups. She tugged the buttercup lights lower, then higher. Somehow each change made the whole table feel less right.

The sky dimmed. A star appeared. Tansy stared at the table and felt her chest grow tight.

"Nothing matches," she whispered.

At that moment Moss arrived, carrying the clover crumbs in a walnut shell.

"Where shall I put these?" he asked.

Tansy looked at the table and blurted, "Wait."

Moss waited politely on the thyme carpet. Soon Pip fluttered down with a tune on his beak, and Juniper arrived through the rosemary stems with silver water in a shell cup balanced between two rose leaves.

They all stopped when they saw Tansy rushing from one side of the table to the other.

"Are we early?" Pip asked.

"No," said Tansy, "but the petals are all wrong, and the cups are uneven, and the lights are too low unless they are too high."

Juniper blinked her gentle eyes. "The lights look lovely."

"They do not look perfect," Tansy replied.

For a little while, nobody spoke. The buttercup lights swayed softly overhead. One petal drifted from the table and landed at Moss's side. He looked at it as if it were treasure.

"I think this one is beautiful," he said.

Tansy stared. It was the petal with the funny curl.

Pip hopped onto the grass table and looked around. "May I tell you something?"

Tansy nodded, though she still felt cross and worried.

"When I was flying here," Pip said, "I could already see your lights shining behind the rosemary. They looked like a welcome."

Juniper placed her shell cup gently in the center of the table. "And the best feasts are the ones where friends feel glad to arrive."

Moss held up the curled petal. "This one will make a fine crumb plate."

Without another word, he set it down. Pip arranged the clover crumbs on top. Juniper tucked a mint leaf beside the shell cup. Suddenly the table no longer looked like something Tansy had to control. It looked like something her friends were helping to make warm.

Tansy took a deep breath. The rosemary smelled calm and steady. She looked again at the different petals, the not-quite-even cups, the gently swaying lights.

Nothing matched exactly.

Yet everything belonged.

"I think," she said slowly, "I was so busy trying to make it perfect that I forgot it was meant to be shared."

Pip smiled. "That happens."

So the glow-feast began. They ate clover crumbs and blackberry jam from acorn lids. They sipped cool water and listened to Pip's evening song. The buttercup lights floated overhead like tiny moons, and the curled petal turned out to be Moss's favorite plate of all.

When the feast was over, Tansy walked each friend to the rosemary opening. The sky beyond was deep blue, and the first fireflies were beginning to blink.

"Thank you for coming," she said.

"Thank you for welcoming us," said Juniper.

After they left, Tansy returned to the circle alone for a moment. One buttercup light bobbed low enough to brush her hair. She smiled and let it.

The table was not perfect.

It had been better than perfect.

It had been full.
