# Cat Thistle and the Greenhouse Watch

Thistle the striped garden cat preferred moonlit wandering. She liked slipping along fences, inspecting warm bricks, and pausing beneath rosemary bushes as if she were queen of every drifting scent in the night.

So when the gardener left a cracked seed tray in the greenhouse on a chilly evening, Thistle did not expect to be asked to stay beside it.

"The little seedlings may feel the cold through the crack," Sorrel explained. "Would you keep watch until dawn?"

Thistle flicked her tail. "Inside?"

"Inside," said Sorrel.

Thistle looked through the greenhouse glass. It was warm, yes, but quiet in a very still sort of way. She imagined the garden outside, silver and exciting. Watching a tray of sleepy seedlings felt, to Thistle, like an extremely unglorious job.

Still, she gave a small nod.

Inside the greenhouse, the air smelled of damp soil and basil. Moonlight shone through the panes in pale squares. The cracked tray sat on a bench, and beside it lay a woolen scarf the gardener had forgotten. Thistle curled around herself and tried to look as though she had not been given a boring task.

At first she listened crossly to the faint creaks of the greenhouse frame.

Then she noticed one small seedling leaning toward the crack where cool air slipped in.

Thistle rose, sniffed, and saw that the draft was real. Without thinking too much about dignity, she nudged the woolen scarf closer and tucked part of it around the tray.

The little seedling stopped trembling.

After that, Thistle found herself paying closer attention. A dry patch of soil near the corner needed the loose flap of scarf pressed gently beside it. A wobbling pot nearby needed a careful paw to steady it. One adventurous beetle had to be escorted away from tender leaves with a firm but polite nudge.

The night passed in quiet useful moments.

Once, Thistle looked longingly through the glass at the moonlit garden paths. They still looked lovely. But when she turned back and saw the seedlings resting safely in their patched-up warmth, a different kind of satisfaction settled in her chest.

This was not an exciting job.

It was an important one.

Near dawn, the greenhouse glass pearled with mist. The tray remained warm under the tucked scarf, and the seedlings stood small but steady in their rows.

Sorrel returned with first light and found Thistle sitting upright beside them like a striped statue.

"You stayed," Sorrel said.

Thistle tried to look casual. "There was work to do."

Sorrel smiled. "And you did it kindly."

When the greenhouse door finally opened and morning air drifted in, Thistle stepped outside into the fresh light. The paths were there for wandering later. For now she was content to stretch in the herb bed and close her eyes.

She had guarded something small through the whole night.

Quiet caretaking, she discovered, could feel every bit as noble as moonlit roaming.
