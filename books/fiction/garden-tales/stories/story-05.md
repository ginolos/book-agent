# Unicorn Juniper and the Pearl Cobwebs

Before dawn, the rose arbor was the quietest place in the whole garden. The petals were still folded inward, holding the last cool bits of night. Pearl cobwebs draped between the stems and shimmered faintly whenever a breeze passed through them. Even the air seemed to whisper instead of speak.

Juniper the young unicorn loved this hour because it felt like a secret. Her coat was as pale as cream, and her small horn glowed with a silver sheen when she sang. She had discovered that certain notes could make the roses stir just before morning.

This pleased her very much.

One morning she decided she would wake every rose in the arbor herself.

"A grand dawn song," she whispered. "That is what this garden needs."

She stood at the center of the arch, lifted her head, and let out the brightest horn-song she could manage.

The sound rang clear as glass.

It was so bright, in fact, that the pearl cobwebs trembled, a sleepy sparrow fluffed up in surprise, and one half-open rose snapped shut again as if it had pulled a blanket over its face.

Juniper blinked. "Oh."

She tried a second note, softer, then another strong one. More webs trembled. A shower of cool droplets pattered down. The roses did not open. If anything, they seemed to tuck themselves more tightly closed.

From below came a small voice. "Perhaps they are still resting."

It was Tansy the fairy, perched on a rose stem.

"But the sky is changing," Juniper said. "I thought I should help."

Tansy touched one folded petal gently. "Helping is lovely. Startling is different."

Juniper felt heat rise in her cheeks. She had only wanted to do something beautiful. Now she worried she had been noisy and showy instead.

So she lowered her head and listened.

The world was not ready all at once. A blackbird had not yet started its first song. The eastern sky was only beginning to pale. A droplet slipped from one cobweb to another with a tiny silver tap.

Juniper took a slow breath.

Then she tried again, but this time her song was no brighter than the color of the sky just before sunrise. It curled through the rose arbor like a ribbon of mist. It did not push. It invited.

Nothing happened at first.

Then one rose loosened.

Another opened one petal. A third lifted its head as if peeking from under the covers. The pearl cobwebs glowed, not with shaking surprise now, but with calm reflected light.

Juniper's eyes widened. She sang one more note, soft and warm.

Around her, the roses began to wake in their own time.

They did not spring open all together. They stretched into morning one by one, the way children rise from bed when the room is gentle and safe. The whole arbor slowly filled with fragrance.

Tansy smiled. "There. Now they can hear your kindness."

Juniper let the last note fade. "I thought strength had to be bright."

"Sometimes," said Tansy, "strength is knowing how softly to use it."

By the time the sun touched the garden wall, the rose arbor was awake. Juniper walked through it with careful steps, admiring the opened blooms and the pearly cobwebs still strung between them like fine necklaces.

She did not feel less magical because her song had been gentle.

She felt wiser.

And when the day fully arrived, the roses seemed to glow in quiet thanks, as if they had not merely been awakened, but greeted.
